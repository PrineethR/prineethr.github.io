naptick

ux | industrial design | physical interaction • 2023

Introduction

The Naptick Sleep Experience Device represented a 4-month intensive design challenge where I led UX and industrial design for a sleep enhancement product. The device combines light therapy, ambient sound, and voice assistance in a form factor designed for the bedside environment. My brief was to create an experience that would serve users across their entire sleep cycle – from evening wind-down through morning wake-up.

The primary design challenge centered on crafting interactions that would be intuitive during moments of reduced cognitive function (when half-asleep) while maintaining aesthetic appeal when inactive. This required balancing competing needs: simplicity of interaction, quality of light/sound, manufacturing feasibility, and cost constraints for our 200-unit friends & family release.

My role encompassed user research, industrial design, physical interaction design, and digital experience development – essentially shepherding the product from concept through to production-ready prototypes.

Discovery & Research

I applied a multi-faceted research approach to understand the sleep technology space:

Competitive Analysis
Evaluated key players (Philips Wake-Up Light, Hatch Restore, Google Nest Hub) to identify opportunity gaps. I found most devices excelled either at light therapy or sound, but rarely both, and many lacked intuitive physical controls.
Contextual Interviews
Conducted 12 in-home interviews observing nightstand setups, morning/evening routines, and existing product interactions. Key finding: users often interacted with devices while in various states of consciousness, requiring extremely intuitive controls.
Technical Exploration
Collaborated with engineering to test various LED arrays, diffuser materials, and speaker configurations, evaluating performance and manufacturing viability.

Design Principles

Elliptical Harmony: Using flowing, continuous forms that feel organic rather than technical

Ambient Presence: Creating a device that visually recedes when not in active use

Gestural Simplicity: Ensuring primary functions are accessible through intuitive physical interactions

Adaptive Response: Designing the device to respond appropriately to different states of user consciousness

Industrial Design Process

The industrial design process focused on creating a form that would support both functional requirements and emotional connection:

1
Initial Form Exploration
I sketched over 30 initial concepts, then narrowed to four distinct approaches:
  • Vertical stacked forms (inspired by traditional bedside lamps)
  • Tapered cone variations (optimizing light projection)
  • Faceted geometric forms (creating interesting light/shadow play)
  • Organic, curved profiles (referencing the Naptick logo's flowing curve)
2
Prototype Iteration
Created five scaled foam models to test proportions and physical presence, followed by three functional prototypes testing different:
  • Light diffusion methods (internal reflection vs. direct projection)
  • Material combinations (opacity, transmission, finish)
  • Button mechanisms (capacitive, mechanical, hybrid)
3
Material Development
The material selection process required balancing aesthetic goals with light performance:
  • Tested seven diffuser materials, measuring light quality and visual appeal
  • Conducted blind user tests to determine preferred light quality
  • Created finish samples to validate manufacturing capability at scale
4
Final Design Refinement
Through this process, I refined the design to a form that balanced technical requirements with aesthetic goals:
  • A gently tapered cylindrical form (200mm height, 150mm base) that provided optimal light projection while maintaining stability
  • Distinct material transitions that doubled as intuitive grip points
  • A weighted base with integrated cable management
  • A distinctive light diffusion pattern that referenced the brand's flowing curve

Physical Interaction Design

For the physical interaction design, I focused on creating a system that would serve users in various states of consciousness – from fully alert to barely awake:

critical interaction mapping
i mapped the full sleep experience to identify key moments requiring physical interaction:
  • evening wind-down (setting sleep timer, adjusting light choreography and sound atmosphere)
  • middle-of-night disruptions (subtle light activation, ambient sound adjustment)
  • morning wake-up (gentle transition, alarm dismissal, progressive light intensity)
light choreography
designed responsive light behaviors that support the sleep journey:
  • evening sequences that gradually shift color temperature from energizing to calming
  • nighttime illumination providing just enough visibility without disrupting melatonin production
  • morning wake-up choreography mimicking sunrise with natural color progression
  • emotional light patterns that communicate device state through subtle pulsing and transitions
sound design
created a comprehensive sound experience that complements the sleep cycle:
  • ambient soundscapes with generative compositions that evolve without repetitive patterns
  • interaction sounds providing subtle feedback without jarring the user from their relaxed state
  • morning audio wake-up designed with gradual intensity increase matched to light changes
  • acoustically engineered response patterns that feel natural and intuitive to half-conscious users
sensory experience design

i developed a cohesive multi-sensory approach that respects users' cognitive states:

  • textured physical controls with distinctive tactile feedback for intuitive nighttime interaction
  • tangible interaction points positioned at natural grip locations on the device surface
  • audio feedback tones specially designed to be recognizable yet gentle when users are half-asleep
  • integrated light and sound experiences that work in harmony to create appropriate sleep or wake atmospheres

UX Strategy & Digital Integration

The physical product needed to work in concert with digital touchpoints, creating a cohesive ecosystem:

Cross-Platform Experience Mapping
Defined which interactions belonged where:
  • Physical product: Time-sensitive, frequent, and simple interactions
  • Mobile app: Complex settings, scheduling, and personalization
  • Voice: Convenience functions and accessibility support
Contextual Experience Design
The system needed to adapt based on context:
  • Time-aware responses (different behaviors morning vs. evening)
  • Ambient light sensing to adjust brightness
  • Gradual transitions between states to avoid jarring experiences
Information Architecture
Created a streamlined app structure with three primary sections:
  • Sleep setup (evening routines, sounds, schedules)
  • Wake up (alarm settings, light patterns, intensities)
  • Settings (device management, preferences)
Interaction Continuity
Designed handoff moments between physical and digital:
  • Physical button press could trigger app options when appropriate
  • App settings directly reflected in physical product behavior
  • Consistent terminology and mental models across touchpoints

outcomes
and
learnings

the naptick project revealed profound insights about designing for vulnerable states of consciousness. moving between research, prototyping, and refinement uncovered how deeply personal our relationship with sleep technology can be. the tactile quality of physical interfaces became not just a functional consideration but an emotional anchor for users navigating the liminal spaces between wakefulness and sleep.

perhaps most significant was discovering how design decisions ripple through the sleep experience ecosystem. the careful crafting of transitions—both in the digital interface and physical product—created moments of genuine connection with users. this project reinforced my belief that thoughtful design can transform our relationship with technology into something that enhances rather than disrupts our natural rhythms.
